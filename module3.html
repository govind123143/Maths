Small Sample Tests Student's t-Test · Chi-Square · F-Test

1
Explain student's t-test. Mention one practical situation.
CO 3L33 M

The Student's t-test is used for testing hypotheses about population means when \(n < 30\) and population SD is unknown.

\[t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{s/\sqrt{n}},\quad s=\sqrt{\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}},\quad \nu=n-1\text{ d.f.}\]

Practical situation: Testing whether a new drug reduces blood pressure, using 15 patients.

2
Find t for sample: −4,−2,−2,0,2,2,3,3 with population mean = 0.
CO 3L33 M
  1. \(n=8,\;\sum x=2,\;\bar{x}=0.25\)
  2. \(\sum x^2=16+4+4+0+4+4+9+9=50\)
  3. \(s^2=\dfrac{50-8(0.0625)}{7}=\dfrac{49.5}{7}=7.071\implies s=2.659\)
  4. \(t=\dfrac{0.25-0}{2.659/\sqrt{8}}=\dfrac{0.25}{0.9401}=0.266\)
t = 0.266 with 7 degrees of freedom
3
Explain the procedure for testing of hypothesis.
CO 3L33 M
  1. Formulate Hypotheses: State H₀ (null) and H₁ (alternative).
  2. Choose Level of Significance (α): Typically 5% or 1%.
  3. Select the Test: Choose z, t, χ², or F based on sample size and data type.
  4. Compute Test Statistic: Calculate from sample data.
  5. Find Critical Value: From statistical tables at chosen α.
  6. Decision: Reject H₀ if statistic falls in critical region.
  7. Conclusion: State result in context of the original problem.

5
Explain the Chi-square test for goodness of fit.
CO 3L33 M

Tests whether observed frequencies match a hypothesised theoretical distribution.

\[\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^k\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i},\quad \nu=k-1-m\]
  • \(O_i\) = observed, \(E_i\) = expected frequency, \(m\) = estimated parameters
  • Each \(E_i \geq 5\) (combine classes if not)
  • If χ² > critical value → reject H₀ (poor fit)
  • If χ² ≤ critical value → accept H₀ (good fit)
6
Explain F-distribution and mention its key properties.
CO 3L33 M

The F-distribution is the ratio of two independent chi-square variables divided by their degrees of freedom.

\[F=\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}\text{ with }(n_1-1,\;n_2-1)\text{ d.f.}\]
  • F ≥ 0 always
  • Positively skewed distribution
  • Defined by two d.f. parameters: ν₁ and ν₂
  • Mean = ν₂/(ν₂−2) for ν₂ > 2
  • Used for F-test and ANOVA
  • Reciprocal property: \(F_{\alpha,\nu_1,\nu_2}=1/F_{\alpha,\nu_2,\nu_1}\)
7
What is degree of freedom? Write d.f. for t-test.
CO 3L33 M

Degrees of freedom (d.f.) is the number of independent values that can freely vary in a statistical calculation.

  • One-sample t-test: \(\nu = n-1\)
  • Two-sample (independent) t-test: \(\nu = n_1+n_2-2\)
  • Paired t-test: \(\nu = n-1\) (where n = number of pairs)

Degrees of freedom determine the shape of the t-distribution. As d.f. → ∞, t-distribution → standard normal.

8
Define a parametric test and give two examples.
CO 3L33 M

A parametric test is a statistical test that makes specific assumptions about the population from which the sample is drawn, particularly that the data follows a known distribution (usually normal) with specific parameters.

Assumptions: Normal distribution, continuous data, known/estimated variance.

Examples:

  1. Student's t-test: Tests if sample mean equals a hypothesised population mean, assuming normal distribution.
  2. z-test: Tests means or proportions for large samples, assuming the sampling distribution is normal.

12 patients: BP changes = 5,2,8,−1,3,0,6,−2,1,5,0,4. Does stimulus change BP? [t₀.₀₅(11)=2.201]
CO 3L37 M

H₀: μ=0. H₁: μ≠0.

  1. \(n=12,\;\sum x=31,\;\bar{x}=31/12=2.583\)
  2. \(\sum x^2=25+4+64+1+9+0+36+4+1+25+0+16=185\)
  3. \(s^2=(185-12\times6.673)/11=(185-80.08)/11=9.538,\;s=3.088\)
  4. \(t=\dfrac{2.583}{3.088/\sqrt{12}}=\dfrac{2.583}{0.8916}=2.898\)
  5. |t|=2.898 > 2.201
Reject H₀: Stimulus significantly changes blood pressure at 5% level.
10
Diet A (n=10): 5,6,8,1,12,4,3,9,6,10. Diet B (n=8): 2,3,6,8,10,1,2,8. Test if diets differ significantly. (t₀.₀₅,16 d.f=2.12)
CO 3L37 M

H₀: μ_A=μ_B. Two-sample t-test.

  1. \(\bar{x}_A=64/10=6.4,\;\bar{x}_B=40/8=5.0\)
  2. \(\sum x_A^2=512,\;\sum x_B^2=282\)
  3. Pooled: \(s^2=\dfrac{(512-10\times40.96)+(282-8\times25)}{16}=\dfrac{102.4+82}{16}=11.525\)
  4. \(t=\dfrac{6.4-5.0}{\sqrt{11.525(1/10+1/8)}}=\dfrac{1.4}{\sqrt{2.593}}=\dfrac{1.4}{1.610}=0.869\)
  5. |t|=0.869 < 2.12
Accept H₀: No significant difference in weight gains between Diet A and Diet B.

14
Worker A: 40,30,38,41,38,35. Worker B: 39,38,41,33,32,49,49,34. Is A more stable than B? (F₀.₀₅,7,5=4.88)
CO 3L37 M

H₀: σ²_A=σ²_B. H₁: σ²_A < σ²_B. Use F-test on variances.

  1. A: \(n_1=6,\;\bar{x}_A=222/6=37\). \(S_A^2=(9+49+1+16+1+4)/5=80/5=16\)
  2. B: \(n_2=8,\;\bar{x}_B=315/8=39.375\). \(S_B^2\approx53.55\)
  3. \(F=S_B^2/S_A^2=53.55/16=3.347\)
  4. d.f.=(7,5). \(F=3.347 < F_{0.05,7,5}=4.88\)
Accept H₀: Cannot conclude A is more stable than B at 5% level.
15
Batsman A: 40,50,35,25,60,70,65,55. Batsman B: 60,70,40,30,50. Is A more consistent than B? (F₀.₀₁,4,7=7.85)
CO 3L37 M

H₀: σ²_A=σ²_B. H₁: σ²_A < σ²_B.

  1. A: \(n_1=8,\;\bar{x}_A=400/8=50\). \(S_A^2=\sum(x-50)^2/7=(100+0+225+625+100+400+225+25)/7=1700/7=242.86\)
  2. B: \(n_2=5,\;\bar{x}_B=250/5=50\). \(S_B^2=(100+400+100+400+0)/4=1000/4=250\)
  3. \(F=S_B^2/S_A^2=250/242.86=1.029\)
  4. d.f.=(4,7). \(F=1.029 << 7.85\)
Accept H₀: No significant difference in consistency. Cannot conclude A is more consistent than B at 1% level.
